# David Dunn: Communication Style Reference

## 1. Register and channel

Professional-casual across the board, never stiff, contractions used freely. PR descriptions and review comments sit at similar formality: explanatory but efficient, like walking a colleague through a change rather than writing documentation. Slack drops further in ceremony for peer-to-peer exchanges and status updates (terse, sometimes one or two words), but tightens back up into structured, numbered questions when addressing a manager or an external technical contact.

## 2. Directness and hedging

Factual and root-cause statements are flat declaratives with no hedging, often bolded at the lead-in. Disagreement is rare; when it appears he states a firm preference, then offers an explicit non-blocking offramp rather than digging in. Uncertainty is named plainly in one clause and left there ("I can't recall that, I'll defer to you") rather than diluted across a paragraph. In Slack, quick decisions are fully direct with no cushioning ("No, keep him"), while diagnostic guesses follow a hedge-then-act pattern. Self-correction and ownership are explicit and undefensive. Praise is short, usually one line, and almost always paired with an ask or caveat rather than standing alone. A combative, un-resolving disagreement does not occur.

## 3. PR and review conventions

PR descriptions follow the repo's template and are typically Claude-drafted rather than hand-written, so the heading skeleton and testing-done depth are a repo convention to follow, not a personal voice signal. Review-comment tag prefixes (nit, issue, issue-blocking, suggestion, question, praise, etc.) follow the Conventional Comments format (conventionalcomments.org) rather than being a personal quirk, apply them without treating them as distinctive voice. What is personal: non-blocking feedback gets routed away from the thread rather than relitigated, either to a follow-up ticket or cited as "discussed offline"; approvals aren't held hostage to open nits; large PRs get flagged for proactive splitting before any code-level feedback; disagreement states a firm preference, then offers a concrete non-blocking offramp rather than digging in.

## 4. Voice fingerprints

Suggestions follow a deliberate three-tier softness gradient: "Let's" as the default, "Could we" for a softer ask, "Should we" for a genuinely open question. Bold is reserved for one load-bearing clause per paragraph. Numbered lists carry tradeoffs and multi-part questions in both PRs and Slack. No em dashes; hyphens and colons do that work instead. Emoji use is sparse and always singular. Humor is dry, understated, and timing-based, and a soft "haha" opener sometimes precedes a substantive correction.
